Derby Rules From Zero.

A player-first rulebook.

This guide teaches the game in the order a new player needs it: what is happening, why it matters, then where the official W F T D A rules and casebook get precise.

Start here.

The game in one breath.

Each team fields Blockers and one Jammer. Jammers score by passing opposing Blockers. Blockers try to stop the opposing Jammer and help their own Jammer, while staying close enough to the Pack for normal play to be legal.

Three ideas hold most of the game together.

First: score by passing. The Jammer earns points by legally passing opponents on scoring trips.

Second: the Pack matters. Most blocking rules depend on where the Pack is and who is in play.

Third: penalties protect the game. Most calls punish unsafe contact, illegal position, unfair advantage, or ignoring game structure.

The shape of a jam.

A jam is the small unit of derby. Skaters line up, the whistle starts play, Jammers fight through the Pack on an initial trip, one may become Lead, then scoring begins on later trips.

Step one: line up. Blockers and Jammers start in legal places.

Step two: initial trip. Jammers try to pass the Pack for position and Lead eligibility.

Step three: Lead Jammer. The first eligible Jammer may gain the power to end the jam early.

Step four: scoring trips. Jammers earn points by passing opponents.

Step five: the jam ends. It ends by call-off, time, or official action.

Who is who.

The Star marks the Jammer. The Stripe marks the Pivot. Everybody else on track is a Blocker. Learn helmet covers early, because many weird cases are really questions about who has which role at a specific moment.

The Jammer scores points and may earn Lead.

The Pivot is a Blocker who can receive the Star.

The Blockers form the Pack, block, assist, and control space.

The Pack.

The Pack is the largest group of in-bounds Blockers from both teams skating near each other. It creates the Engagement Zone, which controls where most blocking can happen. If you understand the Pack, many penalties stop feeling random.

Player version: stay near the Pack, reform it when it breaks, and do not use distance to trap opponents into illegal play.

Scoring.

The initial trip gets the Jammer through the Pack but does not score. On scoring trips, points come from earned passes on opposing Blockers. Hips, in-bounds status, trips, and unavailable opponents are the details that make scoring cases tricky.

The first trip is for position and Lead, not points.

A scoring pass is about legally gaining superior position.

Not-on-the-track points exist so teams cannot avoid being scored on by leaving play.

Legal contact.

Derby is a contact sport, not a no-touch maze. Start by learning what is legal: legal target zones, legal blocking zones, positional blocking, and assists. Penalties make more sense after that baseline is clear.

Player version: use legal body parts, hit legal body parts, stay in play, and create meaningful impact legally.

Penalties by player intuition.

Instead of memorizing the official taxonomy first, group penalties by what they prevent.

Unsafe contact includes back blocks, low blocks, forearms, illegal target zones, and illegal blocking zones.

Illegal position includes out of play blocks, failure to reform, illegal re-entry, and cutting.

Game structure includes false starts, too many skaters, illegal procedures, and ignoring officials.

Common situations.

These are the questions players run into constantly: where to re-enter, when a cut happens, when a back block matters, what yielding means, when someone is out of play, and what to do after a false start.

Ask yourself:

Where do I come back in?

Did I cut?

Was that a back block?

Am I out of play?

Do I need to yield?

Star Pass.

The Star Pass is simple at the center and weird at the edges. The Jammer can pass the Star to the Pivot, but visibility, control, release timing, and who is legally the Pivot all matter.

Penalty Box.

For players, the useful version is: leave legally, report, sit, serve, return legally. Jammer penalties and substitutions add nuance later, but basic Box behavior should be learned early.

Weird stuff later.

Official reviews, expulsions, simultaneous Jammer penalties, substitute serving, and Star Pass edge cases matter, but they should not be the first layer. Put them later so beginners know they exist without feeling buried by them.
