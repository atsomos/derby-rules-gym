Start here

The game in one breath

Each team fields Blockers and one Jammer. The Jammer scores by passing opposing Blockers. The Blockers stop the other Jammer, help their own Jammer, and stay close enough to the Pack for normal play to be legal.

Score by passingPoints come from legal passes on scoring trips, not from the first trip through the Pack.
The Pack mattersThe Pack tells you where most blocking is legal and who is close enough to keep playing.
Penalties protect the gameMost calls are about unsafe contact, illegal position, unfair advantage, or broken game structure.

First mental model

The shape of a jam

A jam is one short burst of derby. Skaters line up, the whistle starts play, Jammers fight through the Pack on an initial trip, one may become Lead, and scoring begins on later trips.

  1. Line up. Blockers and Jammers start in legal places.
  2. Initial trip. Jammers try to pass the Pack for position and Lead eligibility, not points.
  3. Lead Jammer. The eligible Jammer who earns Lead can end the jam early.
  4. Scoring trips. Jammers earn points by legally passing opponents.
  5. Jam ends. It ends by call-off, time, or official action.

People on track

Who is who

The Star marks the Jammer. The Stripe marks the Pivot. Everybody else on track is a Blocker. Learn the helmet covers early, because many weird cases are really questions about who had which role at one exact moment.

Jammer can score points and may earn Lead. Pivot is still a Blocker, but can receive the Star. Blockers form the Pack, block, assist, and control space.

The hard idea

The Pack

The Pack is the largest group of in-bounds Blockers from both teams who are close enough to each other. It creates the Engagement Zone, which controls where most blocking can happen. Once you can find the Pack, many penalties stop feeling random.

Player version Stay near the Pack, reform it when it breaks, and do not use distance as a trick to trap opponents into illegal play.

How points happen

Scoring

The initial trip gets the Jammer through the Pack but does not score. On scoring trips, points come from earned passes on opposing Blockers. Hips, boundary status, trip status, and unavailable opponents are the details that make scoring cases tricky.

  • The first trip is for position and Lead, not points.
  • A scoring pass is about legally gaining superior position, usually with the hips ahead.
  • Not-on-the-track points exist so teams cannot avoid points by leaving play.

Legal before illegal

Legal contact

Derby is a contact sport, not a no-touch maze. Start by learning what legal contact looks like: legal targets, legal blocking zones, positional blocking, and assists. Penalties make more sense after that baseline is clear.

Player version Use legal body parts, hit legal body parts, stay in play, and make your impact legally.

Why calls exist

Penalties by player intuition

Instead of memorizing the official taxonomy first, group penalties by what they are trying to prevent.

Unsafe contactHits to illegal places, hits with illegal body parts, or contact that creates unsafe impact.
Illegal positionBlocking out of play, failing to reform, re-entering illegally, or cutting the track.
Game structureFalse starts, too many skaters, illegal procedures, or ignoring official instructions.

Most useful first

Common situations

These are the questions players run into constantly: where to re-enter, when a cut happens, when a back block matters, what yielding means, when someone is out of play, and what to do after a false start.

Where do I come back in? Did I cut? Was that a back block? Am I out of play? Do I need to yield?

Role changes

Star Pass

The Star Pass is simple at the center and weird at the edges. The Jammer can pass the Star to the Pivot, but the real question is exactly when the Pivot legally becomes the new Jammer.

Serving time

Penalty Box

For players, the useful version is: leave legally, report, sit, serve, return legally. Jammer penalties and substitutions add nuance later, but basic Box behavior should become automatic early.

Not day one

Weird stuff later

Official reviews, expulsions, simultaneous Jammer penalties, substitute serving, and Star Pass edge cases matter, but they should not be the first layer. Keep them here so beginners know they exist without being buried by them.
